Human Factors & Aeromedical

The ways the body and mind can mislead a pilot — and how to stay ahead of them.

Oxygen & hypoxia

Hypoxia (oxygen starvation) can begin to impair you above ~8,000 ft. Symptoms: euphoria, poor judgment, blue fingertips/lips, tunnel vision.

Types of hypoxia

Hypoxic (high altitude) · Hypemic (blood can't carry O₂ — e.g. carbon monoxide) · Stagnant (poor circulation) · Histotoxic (alcohol/drugs block cell use).

Carbon monoxide

Carbon monoxide (CO) poisoning → loss of muscular power, plus headache, dizziness and nausea. It's odorless and usually enters the cabin through a leak in the exhaust-fed cabin heater. Suspect it if the heater is on and you feel unwell — shut off the heater, open vents, and use oxygen.

Night vision

At night it's harder to see things in the center of your vision — the center of the eye is poor in low light. Use off-center (peripheral) viewing and scan; let your eyes dark-adapt (~30 minutes) and avoid bright white light.

Illusions & disorientation

  • Haze makes objects appear farther away than they are — you may fly a lower-than-normal approach.
  • In IMC / instrument conditions, pilots can interpret acceleration as a climb and deceleration as a descent (the somatogravic illusion). Trust the instruments, not your inner ear.
  • A narrow or up-sloping runway can make you feel too high — so you fly a low approach, risking striking obstacles or landing short; a wide, down-sloping, or brightly lit runway can make you feel too low (so you fly too high).

Fitness to fly

IMSAFE — self-assessment checklist
  • I — Illness
  • M — Medication
  • S — Stress
  • A — Alcohol (8 hrs & <0.04 BAC — see Regs)
  • F — Fatigue
  • E — Emotion / Eating (stay nourished & hydrated)
