ACS Extras

The ACS is the blueprint the PAR written test is built from. The other topic pages already cover most of it — this page collects the ACS‑tested knowledge that isn't elsewhere on the site (and isn't just common sense).

Airworthiness extras

  • ADs vs. SAIBs — Airworthiness Directives are mandatory (they fix an unsafe condition); a Special Airworthiness Information Bulletin (SAIB) is advisory only — recommended, not required.
  • Special flight permit — a "ferry permit" from the FAA to fly an aircraft that doesn't currently meet airworthiness but is safe for the flight (e.g., to get to a shop for repairs or an overdue inspection).
  • MEL vs. KOEL — a Minimum Equipment List is an FAA‑approved list that lets you fly with specific items inoperative (and replaces the §91.213(d) flow if you have one); the Kinds of Operation Equipment List (in the POH) shows what's required for VFR/IFR/day/night.

Flight‑plan extras

  • VFR flight plan — activate & close — a VFR flight plan isn't required, but it triggers search and rescue. You must activate it with Flight Service after departure (ATC/tower won't do it) and close it on arrival — if you don't close within 30 minutes of your ETA, SAR procedures begin.
  • Inflight intercept procedures — if intercepted: follow the interceptor, try to contact ATC and monitor 121.5, and answer its signals (rocking wings = "you've been intercepted").

Airspace extras

Beyond the classes & special‑use areas on the Airspace page, the ACS also tests these "other airspace areas":

  • TFR — Temporary Flight Restriction: a short‑notice no‑fly bubble for VIP movement, disasters, wildfires, or major sporting events (check NOTAMs).
  • SFRA — Special Flight Rules Area (e.g., the Washington, DC SFRA): requires a flight plan, a discrete transponder code, two‑way comms, and special training.
  • MTR — Military Training Routes: high‑speed, low‑altitude military routes. IR = IFR, VR = VFR; 4‑digit = at or below 1,500 ft AGL, 3‑digit = segments above 1,500 ft AGL.

Aeronautical decision‑making (ADM)

Risk management uses situational awareness, problem recognition, and good judgment to reduce each flight's risks.

Runway status lights (RWSL)

Runway Status Lights are automatic, red, and fully independent of ATC — they sense traffic and warn you it's unsafe:

  • REL (Runway Entrance Lights) — red at a taxiway/runway entrance: do not enter or cross.
  • THL (Takeoff Hold Lights) — red in the runway: do not start the takeoff roll.
  • A red light overrides an ATC clearance — stop, then confirm with ATC.

Diversion & lost procedures

Diversion

Decide early and turn toward the alternate first, then work out the heading, time, and fuel (estimate — don't bury your head in the chart). Pick the nearest suitable airport and use ATC/EFB as resources.

Lost

The 5 C's: Climb (better view & radio/nav reception), Communicate (ATC or 121.5), Confess (you're lost), Comply, Conserve fuel. Squawk 7700 if it becomes an emergency.

Spin, unusual‑attitude & IMC recovery

PARE — spin recovery
  • P — Power to idle
  • A — Ailerons neutral
  • R — Rudder full opposite the spin
  • E — Elevator forward to break the stall — then recover from the dive
Unusual attitude recovery

Nose‑high (approaching a stall): add power, lower the nose, level the wings. Nose‑low (speed building / spiral): reduce power, level the wings, then gently raise the nose (level wings before pulling, to avoid overstress).

Inadvertent IMC — IIMC/UIMC

Continued VFR into IMC is a top killer. If you lose visual references: trust the instruments, make a 180° level turn to retreat to the VMC you came from (climb if terrain requires), and get help (ATC or 121.5). Avoid abrupt control or head movements (spatial disorientation).
