Human Factors & Aeromedical

The ways the body and mind can mislead a pilot — and how to stay ahead of them.

Oxygen & hypoxia

Hypoxia (oxygen starvation) can begin to impair you above ~8,000 ft. Symptoms: euphoria, poor judgment, blue fingertips/lips, tunnel vision.

Types of hypoxia

Hypoxic (high altitude) · Hypemic (blood can't carry O₂ — e.g. carbon monoxide) · Stagnant (poor circulation) · Histotoxic (alcohol/drugs block cell use).

Time of useful consciousness (TUC)

The time you can act usefully after losing your oxygen supply — it shrinks fast with altitude: ~20–30 min at 18,000 ft, but only 5–12 min at 20,000 ft.

Hyperventilation

Hyperventilation — breathing too fast or deep (often from stress) — blows off too much CO₂. The symptoms (lightheadedness, tingling fingers/toes, a suffocating feeling) closely mimic hypoxia.

Recovery

Slow your breathing (talk aloud, or breathe into a bag to rebuild CO₂). Because it mimics hypoxia, at altitude ensure 100% oxygen first.

Scuba diving before flying

Climbing to altitude after diving can pull dissolved nitrogen out of solution (decompression sickness). Wait before flight:

  • Flight to cabin altitude up to 8,000 ft: wait 12 hours after a dive that did not require a controlled ascent.
  • Flight above 8,000 ft, or after any dive that required a controlled (decompression) ascent: wait 24 hours.

Carbon monoxide

Carbon monoxide (CO) poisoning → loss of muscular power, plus headache, dizziness and nausea. It's odorless and usually enters the cabin through a leak in the exhaust-fed cabin heater. Suspect it if the heater is on and you feel unwell — shut off the heater, open vents, and use oxygen.

Night vision

At night the center of your vision sees poorly in low light. Use off-center (peripheral) viewing and scan; let your eyes dark-adapt (~30 minutes) and avoid bright white light.

Illusions & disorientation

  • In IMC, acceleration feels like a climb and deceleration like a descent (the somatogravic illusion). Trust the instruments, not your inner ear.
  • The leans — the most common vestibular illusion: after a slow, unnoticed roll you feel like you're banking the other way and may lean to "correct."
  • The Coriolis illusion — an abrupt head movement during a prolonged turn triggers an overwhelming tumbling sensation. Avoid sudden head movements; trust the instruments.
