Airplane Performance & Weight/Balance

What thins out the air and hurts performance, the density-altitude math, and keeping the airplane in balance.

V-speeds

VA (maneuvering speed) depends on weight: heavier → higher VA, lighter → lower VA. (A heavier airplane absorbs gusts before reaching a damaging load factor.)

The four factors affecting performance

All four work by changing air density. Thinner air = worse performance.

1 · Altitude

Lower = better, higher = worse.

2 · Temperature

Lower = better, higher = worse (heat spreads the air out).

3 · Pressure

Higher = better, lower = worse.

4 · Humidity

Lower = better, higher = worse (water molecules are lighter → less dense air).

Pressure systems

High pressure = cool, sinking air (better performance). Low pressure = warm, rising air. Performance is worse on low-pressure and high-humidity days.

Pressure & density altitude

Pressure altitude
Pressure Alt = Indicated Alt + (29.92 − altimeter setting) × 1,000

Or simply set the altimeter to 29.92 and read it.

Density altitude
Density Alt = Pressure Alt + 120 × (OAT − 15 °C)

Density altitude = pressure altitude corrected for non-standard temperature — the altitude the airplane actually "feels."

How density altitude moves

↑ Altitude → ↑ density altitude ↓ Pressure (Hg) → ↑ density altitude ↑ Temperature → ↑ density altitude
High density altitude = worse performance
  • Longer takeoff roll and a slower, shallower climb angle.
  • You lift off at the same indicated airspeed but a higher true airspeed, while the engine and prop make less thrust in thin air.
Rules of thumb
  • ~15 °F above standard ≈ +1,000 ft density altitude (≈ 120 ft per °C).
  • True airspeed increases when it's hot — thinner air means a lower indicated airspeed, so you fly faster (higher TAS) for the same IAS. (Same reason the ASI reads slow up high — see Systems.)
How to read it
  1. Start at the outside air temperature on the bottom scale.
  2. Go up to your pressure-altitude line.
  3. Go right to the first reference line, then follow the guide lines to your weight.
  4. Continue to the next reference line, then parallel the guide lines for the wind component (headwind shortens, tailwind lengthens).
  5. To the last reference line, then up the guide lines for the obstacle height.
  6. Read the required distance off the right-hand scale.

Weight & balance

The center of gravity (CG) should be ahead of the center of pressure (CP) and ahead of the main wheels. A forward CG is more stable; an aft CG is less stable (and can make recovery from stalls/spins difficult).

The W&B math
Moment = Weight × Arm · CG = Total Moment ÷ Total Weight
