Maintenance you may do yourself

Look it up: FAR Part 43, Appendix A

As a private pilot you may perform listed preventive maintenance on an aircraft you own or operate — as long as it isn't flown for hire / under air-carrier rules. Common allowed jobs:

  • Change or replenish engine oil; replace oil & fuel filter elements
  • Replace and service landing-gear tires; grease wheel bearings; add air/oil to struts
  • Replace safety belts
  • Replace bulbs, reflectors & lenses of position/landing lights
  • Clean, gap, or replace spark plugs; replace the battery
  • Replenish hydraulic fluid; simple fabric patches (no rib stitching)
Log it

Every job needs a maintenance-record entry with: a description of the work, the date, and the signature, certificate number, and kind of certificate held by the person approving the work. The annual and 100-hour inspections are not preventive maintenance — those need a mechanic/IA (Regulations · Inspections).

Private-pilot privileges & limits

Look it up: FAR Part 61

You can carry passengers and split costs — but you can't fly for hire.

Sharing cost — the "pro rata" rule

You may split a flight's operating expenses with your passengers, but you must pay at least your proportional share (4 people aboard → you pay ≥ ¼). Only these expenses can be shared: fuel, oil, airport expenditures, and rental fees — and the trip needs a common purpose.

  • No acting as PIC for compensation or hire carrying passengers or property.
  • You may be paid only if the flight is incidental to a business or job — and you carry no passengers/property for hire.
  • Charity flights: you may fly a charitable, nonprofit, or community-event passenger flight where the riders pay/donate to the charity — but you aren't paid (reimbursement is pro-rata only). It must be day VFR, nonstop within a 25 SM radius, and you need at least 500 hours.
  • Change of address: notify the FAA within 30 days — after that you may not exercise your certificate until you do.

Medical classes, flight review, and passenger recency are on Regulations.

Operating rules — the high-yield extras

Look it up: FAR Part 91

Right-of-way, weather minimums, required equipment, cruising altitudes, speed and fuel live on Regulations and Airspace. The extras most likely to trip you up:

  • PIC authority: the pilot in command is the final authority for the flight and is responsible for determining the aircraft is airworthy. In an in-flight emergency the PIC may deviate from any rule as needed (reporting rules → Regulations).
  • Inoperative equipment (no MEL): a broken item that isn't required by regulation, your type certificate, or an AD must be removed or deactivated and placarded "INOPERATIVE," and you (or a mechanic) must decide it's not a hazard for the flight.
  • Altimeter: below 18,000 ft, set it to a station within 100 NM along your route.
  • Special VFR: inside a surface-area (Class B/C/D/E) you can request an SVFR clearance from ATC and fly with 1 SM visibility, clear of clouds. At night it requires an instrument rating and an IFR-capable aircraft.
  • Dropping objects: you may drop an object from an aircraft in flight as long as reasonable precautions are taken so it creates no hazard to persons or property.
  • Tower in Class E/G airspace: operating to/from an airport with an operating control tower, establish two-way radio with the tower before 4 NM out and at/below 2,500 ft AGL. (Departing a non-tower satellite airport inside Class C/D? Establish two-way comms with ATC as soon as practicable after takeoff — see ATC.)

Lighting & airport visual aids

Look it up: AIM Chapter 2

Runway markings, PAPI/VASI, and pilot-controlled lighting are on the Airports page. Two more worth knowing:

BeaconMeans
Lighted land airport (civilian)
Lighted water airport
Lighted heliport
Military field — two quick white flashes between greens
Daytime beacon = poor weather

In a Class B/C/D/E surface area, a beacon running in daylight usually means the field is IFR — ground visibility < 3 SM and/or ceiling < 1,000 ft — so an ATC clearance is required to operate there. Don't rely on it alone; still check weather.
