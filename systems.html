Airplane Systems & Instruments

The engine and propeller, the quirks of the magnetic compass, the pitot-static instruments, and the different kinds of altitude.

Propeller & engine

  • Constant-speed propeller = a prop whose blade angle (pitch) can be adjusted to hold a chosen RPM, keeping the engine efficient across speeds. (A fixed-pitch prop cannot.)
  • Carb heat enriches the mixture. Warm air is less dense, so the fuel-to-air ratio goes up (richer). You use carb heat to prevent or melt carburetor ice; expect a small RPM/power drop when it's on.
Its two controls

On a constant-speed prop the throttle sets power output — read on the manifold pressure gauge — and the propeller control regulates engine RPM (on the tachometer).

Detonation

The fuel/air mixture explodes instead of burning smoothly — from fuel grade too low, an over-lean mixture, or excessive engine temps. Fix: richen mixture, reduce power, lower the nose to cool.

Pre-ignition

The mixture ignites too early — before the spark plug fires — usually from a glowing hot spot (carbon/deposit) in the cylinder.

Carb ice watch-out

Carburetor icing is most likely in high humidity with outside temps roughly 20–70 °F — and can occur even on warm days. It does not need visible moisture (unlike airframe icing) — it can form in clear air. First sign with a fixed-pitch prop: a gradual RPM drop.

Hot magneto (P-lead)

Magnetos are self-powered — the ignition switch shuts one off by grounding it through its P-lead. A broken or disconnected P-lead ground means the switch can't ground the mag, so it stays hot and the engine can fire even with the switch OFF. Always treat the prop as live.

Magnetic compass errors

The compass only reads correctly in straight, level, unaccelerated flight. Two mnemonics cover the dynamic errors (Northern Hemisphere):

ANDS

Acceleration error, on E/W headings:

Accelerate → North
Decelerate → South

UNOS

Turning error, rolling out onto N/S:

Undershoot → North (lead)
Overshoot → South (lag)

Why it happens

A small weight offsets magnetic dip, leaving the card slightly unbalanced. In a bank the card tilts and Earth's vertical magnetic pull swings it — turning error; speeding up or slowing down throws that unbalanced card by inertia — acceleration error.

Start vs. end of the turn

UNOS only covers how to roll out (end) a turn onto N/S — not the start. For the start of the turn, use the two-hands technique: stack two hands, and when you bank one way the card's south end swings the other.

At the start of a turn the compass misbehaves near N/S headings — and the effect reverses between hemispheres:

Heading you leaveNorthern HemisphereSouthern Hemisphere
NorthFirst swings the opposite way, then lagsExaggerates the turn (leads)
SouthExaggerates the turn (leads)First swings the opposite way, then lags
Variation vs. deviation

Variation = the angle between true north and magnetic north (varies by location).
Deviation = compass error from metal and electrics in the airplane itself.

Pitot-static system

Three instruments run off air pressure. The pitot tube senses ram (dynamic) pressure; the static port senses ambient (still) pressure.

Blockages & altitude effects

  • Static port blocked: no effect at first — but the moment you change altitude the altimeter freezes at the last reading and the VSI sticks at 0 (airspeed also reads off). Use the alternate static source.
  • Pitot tube blocked: affects only the airspeed indicator.
  • At higher altitude the airspeed indicator reads slower than your true airspeed — fewer air molecules ram into the tube. (Indicated airspeed < true airspeed up high.)
Static + alternate both blocked

Break the glass on the VSI — cabin air then becomes the static source, restoring the altimeter and airspeed indicator. The VSI is sacrificed since it's the least useful.

Airspeeds: IAS → CAS → TAS → GS

Four speeds — each is the one before it with one more correction applied.

IAS — Indicated
What the airspeed indicator reads straight off ram air. This is what you fly — V‑speeds and stall margins are all in indicated airspeed.
CAS — Calibrated
IAS corrected for instrument & position (installation) error — largest at low speed / high angle of attack (e.g., flaps down). Read it from the POH airspeed‑calibration table; near cruise CAS ≈ IAS.
TAS — True
CAS corrected for air density (altitude & temperature) — your real speed through the air mass. Used for performance & flight planning; found on the E6‑B from pressure altitude + OAT.
GS — Ground speed
TAS corrected for wind — your speed over the ground. Drives time en route, fuel burn, and ETA; found on the wind side of the E6‑B.
You always fly indicated

V‑speeds (stall, VA, VNO, VNE) are referenced to indicated airspeed, so the wing stalls at the same IAS at any altitude — even though your TAS and groundspeed are much higher up high.

Quick rules of thumb
  • At sea level on a standard day, IAS ≈ CAS ≈ TAS.
  • TAS rises ~2% per 1,000 ft of altitude for the same IAS (thinner air) — and rises further when it's hot (see density altitude).
  • GS = TAS ± wind: a headwind makes you slower over the ground, a tailwind faster.

Attitude indicator

The attitude indicator is gyroscopic: the gyro holds the horizon bar fixed in space, and the instrument case — with the little reference airplane — moves around it as the aircraft pitches and rolls.

Reading it

Always judge pitch and bank only from the miniature airplane's position relative to the horizon line — not from any other marking. From your seat the little airplane stays put while the horizon rotates around it.
